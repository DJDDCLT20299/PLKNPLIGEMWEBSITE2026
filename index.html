Our Project

Why Terbium & Lanmodulin?

Recovering critical rare earth elements from e-waste using engineered proteins

💡

The Problem

Terbium (Tb) is a critical rare earth element essential for green technologies — LEDs, wind turbines, and electric vehicles. Yet fluorescent lamp e-waste containing Tb is mostly landfilled. Current recovery relies on harsh solvent extraction with poor selectivity.

🧬

Our Solution

We engineered lanmodulin (LanM) — a naturally occurring rare earth-binding protein — with tryptophan sensitizers that enable selective Tb³⁺ capture and detection via luminescence. Our variants bind Tb at pH 2–3 with 99% selectivity over other metals.

🌍

Impact

A green, protein-based alternative to toxic solvent extraction. Our biosensor works at parts-per-billion sensitivity in real e-waste leachate, enabling a circular economy for critical rare earth elements with minimal environmental footprint.

The Science

How Lanmodulin Selectively Binds Terbium

From protein structure to luminescent detection

🔬 EF-Hand Domains

Lanmodulin features four EF-hand calcium-binding motifs that have evolved to bind lanthanides (Ln³⁺) with picomolar affinity — 100 million times tighter than calcium. Each domain coordinates Tb³⁺ via conserved glutamate/aspartate residues.

✨ Tryptophan Sensitization

By engineering tryptophan residues near the binding pocket, we exploit the "antenna effect" — Trp absorbs UV light (280 nm) and transfers energy to bound Tb³⁺, which emits characteristic green luminescence at 545 nm for ultrasensitive detection.

🧪 Acid-Stable Binding

Unlike conventional REE-binding proteins, LanM retains its structure and binding affinity at pH 2–3 — exactly the conditions of e-waste leachate. This allows direct Tb capture from acid-digested fluorescent lamp phosphors without pH adjustment.
