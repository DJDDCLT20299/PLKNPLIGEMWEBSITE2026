Project Description

Engineered lanmodulin variants for selective recovery of terbium from fluorescent lamp e-waste leachate

Background

The Critical Importance of Terbium

Terbium (Tb) is a heavy rare earth element (REE) classified as critical raw material by the European Commission and the US Department of Energy. It is indispensable in modern green technologies: Tb³⁺-doped phosphors enable the bright green emission in trichromatic fluorescent lamps, LEDs, and plasma displays. Terbium is also essential in magnetostrictive alloys (Terfenol-D) used in precision actuators and sensors.

Despite its technological importance, Tb faces severe supply risk. China controls over 85% of global REE refining capacity, and geopolitical tensions frequently disrupt supply chains. Recycling Tb from end-of-life products — particularly fluorescent lamps — could significantly alleviate supply pressure while reducing the environmental burden of mining.

The E-Waste Opportunity

Fluorescent lamps contain up to 15–20% rare earth phosphors by weight, with Tb comprising 5–10% of the phosphor blend. With over 1 billion fluorescent lamps discarded annually worldwide, this represents a substantial secondary source of terbium. Current recycling rates for lamp phosphors remain below 5%, with most ending up in landfills.

The standard recovery method — roasting + HCl leaching + D2EHPA solvent extraction — achieves ~85% Tb leaching but suffers from poor selectivity (final Tb₄O₇ purity of only 64%), high chemical consumption, and generation of toxic organic waste streams. There is a clear need for greener, more selective recovery technologies.

Lanmodulin: Nature's REE Binder

Lanmodulin (LanM) is a recently discovered 12 kDa protein from the methylotrophic bacterium Methylobacterium extorquens. Unlike other metal-binding proteins, LanM has evolved to bind lanthanides (Ln³⁺) with extraordinary affinity — dissociation constants (Kd) in the picomolar to nanomolar range — while binding calcium, its structural homologue, with 10⁸-fold lower affinity. This selectivity arises from its four EF-hand domains, which undergo a conformational change upon Ln³⁺ binding.

Importantly, LanM remains folded and functional under harsh acidic conditions (pH 2–3) typical of e-waste leachate, making it an ideal scaffold for engineering selective Tb biosensors and recovery systems.

The Problem

Current terbium recovery from fluorescent lamp e-waste relies on non-selective solvent extraction using organophosphorus acids (e.g., D2EHPA), which co-extracts other REEs (Eu, Y, La, Ce) resulting in low Tb purity. The process requires multiple stages, large volumes of organic solvents, and generates acidic wastewater. No existing biological method achieves selective Tb capture directly from lamp phosphor leachate at industrially relevant concentrations and pH.

Key Challenge

Develop a protein-based biosensor and capture system that selectively binds Tb³⁺ from complex e-waste leachate containing competing REEs and transition metals, operating at pH 2–3 with ppb-level sensitivity.

Our Solution

🧬 Engineered Lanmodulin Variants

We designed a panel of LanM variants with strategic tryptophan substitutions at positions adjacent to the EF-hand binding pockets. These Trp residues act as "sensitizers" that absorb UV light (280 nm) and transfer energy to bound Tb³⁺, enabling luminescence-based detection without extrinsic dyes.

🔦 Tb-Selective Luminescent Biosensor

When Tb³⁺ binds the engineered binding pocket, tryptophan-sensitized luminescence produces bright green emission at 545 nm. The signal intensity correlates with Tb concentration, providing quantitative detection down to 3 ppb — outperforming current ICP-MS alternatives in cost and portability.

♻️ Recyclable Capture System

Bound Tb³⁺ is released by lowering pH below 2 or adding competing chelators (EDTA/citrate), regenerating the protein for multiple capture cycles. We demonstrate stable performance over 10+ binding-release cycles with minimal loss of activity.

Our approach is a green alternative to solvent extraction: water-based, protein-mediated, with minimal chemical input and no organic waste. By leveraging LanM's natural REE affinity and acid stability, we achieve selective Tb recovery directly from fluorescent lamp phosphor leachate at pH 2.5.
