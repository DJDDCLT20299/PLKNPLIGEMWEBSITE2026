Human Practices

Engaging with stakeholders to shape responsible REE recovery technology

Integrated Human Practices

From the outset, we engaged with stakeholders across the REE value chain — from e-waste recyclers and metallurgists to environmental regulators and policy makers. Their insights fundamentally shaped our project design and goals.

Key Insight

E-waste recyclers emphasized that any biological REE recovery method must work at pH < 3 (typical leachate conditions) and tolerate competing metals. This directly motivated our focus on LanM's acid stability and the tryptophan-sensitized selectivity mechanism.

Stakeholder Engagement

Stakeholder Type Key Feedback Impact on Project
Dr. Joseph Cotruvo — Penn State (LanM discoverer) Academic Expert Recommended EF-hand positions for Trp substitution; shared structural insights on LanM pH stability Informed our N58W design — the Trp position shown to have optimal antenna effect
E-Waste Recyclers Association (HK) Industry "Must work at pH 2–3 without pre-neutralization. Cannot tolerate organic solvents in our facility." Focused our entire approach on acidic, aqueous-phase protein binding. Abandoned organic extraction approaches.
Dr. Koen Binnemans — KU Leuven (REE recycling expert) Academic Expert "Tb/Eu/Dy separation is the hardest challenge. Protein selectivity must exceed 95% to be viable." Set our selectivity target >95%. Achieved 99% Tb selectivity through LanM engineering.
Environmental Protection Department (HK) Regulator "Need to demonstrate that the protein itself is biodegradable and the process generates minimal secondary waste." Added biodegradability assessment. Designed citrate-based elution (fully biodegradable) instead of EDTA.
Critical Materials Institute (Ames Lab) Research Consortium Shared data on industrial REE leachate composition and current D2EHPA performance benchmarks Used real leachate composition data to design our selectivity panel. Benchmarked against D2EHPA performance.

Education & Public Engagement

We organized several outreach activities to raise awareness about REE criticality, e-waste recycling, and synthetic biology solutions:

🏫 School Workshops

Visited 6 local secondary schools with a "Rare Earths in Your Pocket" workshop. Students extracted Tb from crushed fluorescent lamps using our LanM protein and observed the green luminescence under UV light. ~300 students reached.

📱 Social Media Campaign

#LanMTbChallenge on Instagram/TikTok: Followers guessed which everyday items contain terbium. Educational short-form videos on REE mining, supply chains, and bio-recycling. 50K+ cumulative views.

♻️ E-Waste Collection Drive

Partnered with a local e-waste recycler to collect 200 kg of fluorescent lamps from the community. Donated proceeds to a local environmental NGO. Educational booth on REE recycling at the collection site.

📋 Policy Brief

Submitted a policy brief to the Hong Kong Environmental Bureau recommending incentives for biological REE recovery R&D and extended producer responsibility for lamp phosphors.

Sustainable Development Goals

Our project directly addresses the following UN Sustainable Development Goals:

🎯 SDG 9 — Industry, Innovation & Infrastructure

Advancing bio-based REE recovery technology for sustainable industrial processes.

🎯 SDG 11 — Sustainable Cities & Communities

Diverting fluorescent lamp e-waste from landfills through urban mining.

🎯 SDG 12 — Responsible Consumption & Production

Enabling closed-loop recycling of critical materials, reducing primary mining demand.

🎯 SDG 14 — Life Below Water

Reducing REE mining pollution (radioactive thorium tailings, acid mine drainage).
